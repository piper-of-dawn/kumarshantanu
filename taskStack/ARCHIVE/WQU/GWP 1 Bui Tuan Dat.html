$\Large\textbf{Heteroskedascity}$
$\textbf{Definition}$
$$ \mathrm{Var}(e) = \begin{pmatrix} \delta_{1}^{2} & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \delta_{2}^{2} & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \delta_{3} ^{2} & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & \delta_{n} ^{2} \\ \end{pmatrix} $$ where $\mathrm{Var}(e)$ is variance of the residual and $\delta_{1}^{2} \neq \delta_{2}^{2} \neq \delta_{3}^{2} \neq \cdots \neq \delta_{n}^{2}$

$\textbf{Description}$
Heteroskedascity in regression model occur when the variance of the residual (different between predicted value and observed value) is not constant. This would violate one of the most important assumptions of the OLS.

$\textbf{Demonstration}$
The model will clarify the relationship between engine size of a car and fuel consumption on the highway. The OLS model is as follows:
$$ Y_{i} = \beta_{0} + \beta_{1}\times X_{i} + \varepsilon_{i} $$
where:
$Y_{i}$ is fuel consumption on the highway,
$X_{i}$ is size of car's engine,
$\beta_{0}$ is the intercept,
$\beta_{1}$ is the coefficient or slope of regression line,
$\varepsilon_{i}$ is the error term of model
Input data is taken from the following website: https://open.canada.ca/data/en/dataset/98f1a129-f628-4ce4-b24d-6f16bf24dd64

The Breush-Pagan Test is a method to test the heteroskedasticity of the model. When p-value < 0.05, it is possible to reject the hypothesis $H_{0}$: Homoskedasticity exists, or in other words, the model exists with heteroskedasticity.

$\textbf{Damage}$

There are two main reasons to explain why we should correct heteroskedasticity in the model:

If heteroscedasticity exists, the OLS estimators are inefficient because alternate estimators with smaller variances of the regression coefficients that are still unbiased can be found.

If there is heteroscedasticity, the estimators of the standard errors of the regression coefficients are frequently less than reality, giving a false sense of the accuracy of the regression coefficients.

$\textbf{Direction}$

One of the following methods can be used to adjust heteroskedasticity in regression analysis:
1. Make a change to the dependant variable: One method for dealing with heteroskedasticity is to alter the dependent variable in some way. Taking the log of the dependent variable is a typical transformation. This adjustment can assist decrease the data's oscillations between large and small values.
2. Change the definition of the dependent variable: Another method for dealing with heteroskedasticity is to rename the dependent variable. If you wish to anticipate spending on a product, for example, you may use the spend-to-total income ratio rather than the exact spend amount.
3. Make use of weighted regression: Another method for dealing with heteroskedasticity is to employ weighted regression, which assigns weights to data based on their degree of confidence. Weights will be assigned to observations with big mistakes and vice versa.

$\Large\textbf{Kurtosis}$

$\textbf{Definition}$
Kurtosis formula: $$\text{Kurtosis}=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{(X_{i}-\bar{X})^4/N}{s^{4}}$$ where $\bar{X}$ is the mean of $\text{X}$ and $\text{s}$ is the standard deviation of $\text{X}$

$\textbf{Description}$
Kurtosis measures how tall and sharp a probability distribution’s peak is compared to a normal distribution with the same shape. It also tells how heavy the tails of the distribution are. Heavy tails mean high kurtosis.
Kurtosis = 3 $\Leftrightarrow$ mesokurtic,
Kurtosis $\geq$ 3 $\Leftrightarrow$ leptokurtic,
Kurtosis $\leq$ 3 $\Leftrightarrow$ platykurtic,
The chart below depicts 3 types of kurtosis

$\textbf{Damage}$

Excess kurtosis in the OLS error term indicates that there are more outliers or extreme values in the residuals than would be expected under normality. This might imply that several OLS assumptions, such as homoscedasticity, linearity, or error independence, have been broken. Excess kurtosis may also have an impact on the validity of several statistical tests that rely on normality, such as t-tests and F-tests.

$\textbf{Direction}$

If the OLS residual has too much kurtosis, you can try different solutions depending on what causes the problem. Some possible solutions are:

1. Changing the dependent variable or the independent variables with some functions to make their distributions less skewed and less kurtotic. For example, you can use log, square root, or Box-Cox functions.
2. Using other regression methods that are not affected by outliers or extreme values. For example, you can use M-estimators, MM-estimators, or least trimmed squares.
3. Using GLM that can handle different error distributions than normal. For example, you can use a t-distribution or a gamma distribution to model errors with heavy tails.
4. Using bootstrapping methods that do not need normality assumptions and create confidence intervals and p-values by resampling techniques.
